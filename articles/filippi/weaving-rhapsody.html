Weaving my Rhapsody

Essay ~5 min read March 2026
by Matilde Filippi

In my little bedroom, for at least ten years, there have been two Eiffel Towers.

The first is a small and mediocre impressionist painting, a middle-school assignment I was particularly proud of. The second is a 3D puzzle which, during one of those Christmases void of exams, of a car, and of much to do, I had the time to put together.

The tower, a symbol which so loudly screams Paris, used to annoy me because I had nothing to do with that city. I had never been there, I wasn't especially interested in it, and I never got the accents right on French tests (enough for me to mischiefvously declare, "I hate France.")

Curiously, two weeks ago, while walking along the Seine, my bedroom - the museum of my adolescence - came back to mind. With it, the two Towers.

Suddenly, the weaving began.

Suddenly, everything seemed to make sense: that empty symbol had simply been waiting to be filled with meaning. It was simply a matter of time.

I chuckled at how easily one can create a mythology of oneself.

Whispering to myself that everything is connected, that life rhymes with itself, brings me great comfort. I wrap myself in this warm, woven cloak and, for a few moments, the little voice which endlessly asks "Was it the right choice?" falls silent.

— ∿∿∿ —

We are all rhapsodes of ourselves.

Etymology ῥαψῳδός (rhapsōdós) — from Ancient Greek ῥάπτω (rhápto), "to stitch," and ᾠδή (ōidē), "song, poetry." A rhapsode was a professional reciter of epic verse who stitched together stories into a seamless whole.
·     ·     ·

To be honest, I ended up in Paris by chance, to research a topic I had chosen only halfway and had entrusted, for the other half, to the advice of (ahimè) AI.

And yet, in this case as well, the skilled needle somehow found a marvelous way to hold the threads together.

The pretext was the summer afternoons spent on my grandfather's knees, listening to stories of a surreal childhood spent between football games in the courtyard and unexploded bombs on the road home, between a maths lesson and one on how to load a gun, between a carefree theft of cherries and the anxiety that Nazis might burn down your house.

Before realizing that I was interested in history in general, I became obsessed with the years of World War II.

One moment it's Harry Potter and One Direction, the next it's Fascism and the Holocaust.

So, when my research took shape as an exploration of the chaos between the years 1945 and 1950, the puzzle clicked. I could tell myself that this had been waiting for me since childhood.

And here I am, a few months later, intent on untangling the skein of narratives and counter-narratives woven by the postwar press by redrawing the memory of Resistance and of Occupation. All to suit the needs of its propaganda.

The left pulls threads in one direction, the right pulls them in another. The center stitches dull grey patches over everything.

It's a Phoenician weave which I, an expert seamstress, am eager to decipher.
