A Game of Pretense

Article ~12 min read May 2026
by Lorenzo Giovine

Dynamical systems ought to be the most exciting and dull objects in mathematics. A thousand and one models for everything, from lines of cars at a toll booth modelled around Poissonian distributions to the evolution of the Earth's tectonic plates under the effects of mantle convection. Perhaps no dynamical system has seen more mathematics written about it than The Economy (assume a lightning bolt flashing across with roaring thunder as you read this term). And while the numbers chicanery going on at the financial level is no surprise to anyone who knows anything about the last century of market woes, I'd argue that finance is working within the guardrails set by the axioms of most modern free market economics. To make my point clear: it's the very axioms of our models that simplify and plastify our system to a degree where the guardrails can do nothing but guide us serenely to collapse. Over and over, that is.

There is a growing empirical weight to the claim made by the lower echelons of society: that though the stock market may be booming, and though private investment may enjoy more highs than lows, the economic life of the “traditional" First World doesn't feel tethered to the evolution of finance. This disconnect is not a glitch; it is a symptom of a dimensional mismatch. The structural rot in today's economy is tied to a violent reduction in dimensionality. We are attempting to resolve the n-dimensional problems of supply, demand, and material entropy through the narrow opening of sole-and-universal fungibility. By forcing every disparate material reality to be expressed through the single 1D scalar of currency, we have effectively laminated the world. If we treat the economy as a manifold and its actors as nodes, it’s easy to construe why lamination is a fatal flaw of this system: it does not allow for elasticity. Let me go back to my material science metaphor. The economy is an open system, and thus giving elasticity to the manifold that represents it is a fundamental part of the system’s tolerance. Elasticity is the ability to return to stability after stress is applied; it allows for shocks to be absorbed and makes tearing—systemic rupture—unlikely. But at times, currency acts not just as a bottleneck, but as an outright source of needless excess. By its very design, it introduces specific ways of defrauding and profiting at the expense of single nodes, which signifies a change in the curvature of the manifold proper.

In essence, I see currency as the very nature of the connection between nodes; yet, an inadequate currency cannot account for the needs of the entire manifold, simply because of dimensionality.

To make matters worse, the monodimensional nature of currency makes it incredibly unwieldy to counteract the introduction of rigidity. Because the connection is universal, changing its mechanics by rule of law influences the structure of the entire manifold simultaneously, and not changing its mechanics… does nothing! This makes selecting singular areas of rigidity for correction a mathematical nightmare — you cannot soften one part of the cloth without compromising the tension of the whole. If we assume that a higher collective efficiency of this system is a more desirable state at any point in time, then currency and its exchange become an enormous problem. It creates a bottleneck of paths where inefficient routes will be picked, despite the label of market-evolved efficiency, for the simple reason that the currency connection isn't functional enough to map the reality it serves. It introduces curvature, and the lack of direct paths in the exchange of goods makes finding the path of least resistance incredibly difficult. We are left wandering the long way around, while the "juice" of the exchange is harvested by the geometry of the path itself. One of the greatest strengths of mathematics is the ability for multiple models to be simultaneously useful; no model is ever 'correct', but many models tell us something about the system they are used to analyse. At present, I find myself trying to give more rigorous structure to the very economics I want to criticise. But the specific structure that may suit economics is a topic for another article.

p.s. I’d beg the reader to take the word “plastify,” straight out of materials science, in the quite literal sense: “to manipulate and render rigid.” This is not a flowery descriptor for bureaucracy; it is a structural feature of an evolving system. Understanding this transition from the elastic to the rigid will enormously ease comprehension of the mechanical analogy I use down the line.
