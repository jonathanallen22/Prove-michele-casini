Burnout Clarity

Essay ~8 min January 2025
by Michele Mazzocchi
Part I

I sat in a middle seat on the plane to Argentina. I thought I was escaping university burnout: the late nights, exams, the pressure to perform. Shitty lukewarm coffee; clouds moving slowly outside. Just my thoughts, and a sense that nothing really had changed yet.

Buenos Aires: choclo empanadas from a street vendor, the price on a cardboard sign reading "$_00 pesos." The first digit changes often. Then it was the days in brain surgery. I was an intern that followed instructions, observed, tried to keep up. It wasn't bad, but not quite what I needed.

A couple of months before leaving, my friend A. showed me his work on patient genetics and immune response. He would later become my supervisor. I liked the way he thought, and I liked working with him.

Some people fight infections easily, others don't. It's not chance—it's genetics: inborn errors of immunity. That logic stayed with me as I walked through Buenos Aires, ate alfajores, and tried to make sense of being mostly useless in the operating room.

At night, I often ended up in small bars like La Esquinita, cheap fernet in hand. Medicinal in taste, but more importantly, cheap. Inside, I would unconsciously simplify what I saw. A sparse room would become populated in my mind by patterns: a few young doctors drafting futures on napkins, others arguing politics—Milei, public healthcare, the chaos of "obras sociales". But this wasn't really a description of the room. It was a way my mind was trying to structure it. La Esquinita was often almost empty. Those figures did appear, but not as a rule. I was the one arranging fragments into categories, trying to make sense of a system that resisted clarity.

The healthcare system felt chaotic during the day. At night, over cheap fernet, it became even more so—refracted through political argument, half-knowledge, and speculation. Not wrong, but unstable, like the system itself seen through distortion.

I was interested, but out of place. And yet, something was forming: the need for whatever research I would do to carry a social meaning. That still vague, slightly naive impulse eventually pushed me toward working on dengue after the internship ended.

Part II

I've always struggled with non-scientific texts. In school, everyone else relaxed when the exam was reading comprehension, with the answers already sitting in the passage. For me, those exercises were a labyrinth. I'd drift into my own paranoid vortex of hypothesis while the rest of the class cruised.

At some point in my teens, I realised that this "misreading" wasn't such a flaw. It became a weird, persistent source of inspiration.

Take William S. Burroughs. He once said, "language is a virus." When I revisited that line, my misinterpretation took me somewhere useful. What if we were to flip it? What if a virus is a language—like a piece of text embedded in another text—not so different from a genome threaded into a host? Something that slides, mutates, improvises, depending on the "syntax" around it.

Disease isn't a Western-style showdown between good immune cells and evil pathogens. It's none of that silent, dust-thick standoff in red sand. It's a conversation, a network of interactions, a system-level syntax with fluid, forever-changing hierarchical rules. The virus alone isn't the disease. The disease is the virus in conversation with the body's own vulnerabilities.

West Nile, Dengue, or Herpes Simplex are viruses that often linger in the background—almost invisible—until the immune system leaves a door ajar. Congenital immune defects, autoantibodies, missing pattern recognition receptors, dysfunctional cytokine cascades: these vulnerabilities let the viral "text" improvise, accessing tissues it normally wouldn't, shifting tropism, and turning an otherwise subtle presence into severe disease.

That's how HSV, normally stuck to lips or genitals, ends up in the brain or liver. It's how dengue jumps from mild fever to shock or encephalitis. How West Nile, usually silent, produces severe neuroinvasive disease in nearly 40% of hospitalized cases linked to IFN α and IFN ω autoantibodies.

Disease is a network, not a duel.

Viruses mutate, improvise, and communicate within hosts, their scripts shaped by immune vulnerabilities. Charting these interactions makes chaos intelligible—and points to smarter interventions: predictive immunoprofiling, targeted prophylaxis, support for the immune systems most at risk.

Viruses, like language, often adapt to the rules of a system. But, like the schoolchild misreadings that once led me down labyrinths of text, some patterns seem instead to suggest ways of rewriting it.
