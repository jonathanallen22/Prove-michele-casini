I
The Wenxuan

The Wenxuan has a table of contents that would have made Foucault froth uncontrollably. It presents 37 categories of wen, and is edifying enough to be presented here entire.

The 37 categories of wen — hover to illuminate, or illuminate all at once

Note the conflations of this word, wen. Consider that the scholars who compiled and studied from this book found between the Rhapsody on Snow and the Articles of Impeachment against an Inspector of Henan certain differences of form, of content, but certainly none of type; for from the vast body of writing that was available to him, Zhaoming anthologised (xuan: anthology) only what he found most beautiful, — and to say that a lyric must be better literature than an examination question or a declaration of war would only have confused him.

Why should such beautiful articles of impeachment be passed over? Because they were used for something? Are not lyrics used as drinking songs? And did not Old Mei Sheng invent the rhapsody to cure a prince of his madness? — though this last probably isn't true...

II
The Bones

The oldest Chinese writing we have was scratched into the fibulae of oxen and the shells of tortoises. Cast into fire, the bones would fissure (generally a trivalent junction, conf. Chinese 卜, prophecy), and that break was what the seers read. The characters drawn onto the bones we still have were probably written afterwards, to record a prediction, sometimes followed by retrospections refuting or compounding the earlier writing: sometimes this meant material to keep a village entertained for a week or so: 'Sacrifice three sheep to Lady Geng.' Then, 'Sacrifice five sheep to Lady Geng.' Then, 'Seven sheep for Lady Geng!' Then, 'Wait! No sheep for Lady Geng!' Then, 'Wait! Yes! Sheep!'

卜
卜  ·  prophecy  ·  the fissure  ·  the crack

The shaman said:

不食新矣
[You will] not [live]
to eat the new [harvest].

This is, I confess, not direct from a scapula but mediated through the Zuozhuan, itself an expatiation of the Annals of Spring and Autumn. The Annals are very dry; the Zuozhuan is very beautiful. Beautiful and, like Dante, it seems to have brought its age to an end. For the first time there are translucencies, ironies; for the first time, literature becomes delightful. Here are the lords and ministers of a very ancient Celestial Kingdom, drawn clearly, movingly, passing before you like ghosts.

And we can draw an almost vulgarly obvious connection between aphorism and prophecy. Were they literary artefacts? Would the shamans have noticed a difference? Would Zhaoming? Always, always there's something incantatory.

III
The Same Utopia

One of the Bonampak murals in Mexico shows the scribes of a conquered city huddled, squatting, their hands mutilated: presumably the kings of the Classic Maya, their rule dependent not just on the grand old tyranny but also on the rituals (for they were, not are, rituals) of persuasion, took exception to the instruments of others' power. The image is therefore more semantically precise than any of those other gorgeous, frightening victory-murals left by every civilisation that bothered with murals: it represents the erasure of a statal order, — or rather, the production of a statal order.

Were they not all three reaching for the same utopia?

The man for whom China is named ascended the throne and ordered the burning of the books and the burying of the scholars. Zhaoming, who made an anthology, was the crown prince of Liang. He and Qin Shi Huang and that Mayan conqueror, — were they not all three reaching for the same utopia?
