Road to nowhere

Article ~12 min read February 2025
by Olmo Notarianni

In predictive modelling, given an input $x$ — e.g., the number of Japanese cars sold in the United States — we want to predict $y = f(x)$ — e.g., the number of suicides by road accident (picked here). There is no physical model representing this; however, we can approximate an unknown function $f$ describing the relation between $x$ and $y$ given past realizations of these variables. The concept, i.e. the hidden relationship represented by $f$, changes over time. We might expect that due to a presidential election, tariffs on Japanese cars could suddenly increase or suicide rate would decrease because people are too busy keeping up with all the shit going on. The model must be updated to maintain a good approximation over time. You don't know where you are, nor where you are going - probably, it doesn't even matter. It took me a couple of months to understand that.
This was only the beginning. The landscape ran along the strange route Milan-Bucharest-Istanbul-Milan-Sibenik-Sparta, so it seems appropriate to describe the concept on a map.
